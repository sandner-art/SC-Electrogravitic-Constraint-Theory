## 2. Quantum Locking: The Meissner Effect and Flux Pinning

"Quantum locking" is the popular name for the phenomenon of **flux pinning**, and it relies on two distinct quantum-mechanical principles:

### A. The Meissner Effect (Repulsion/Lift)
The Meissner effect occurs when a superconductor is cooled below its critical temperature and completely **expels all magnetic field lines** from its interior.
*   **Mechanism:** This expulsion is a form of perfect diamagnetism. The superconductor generates surface currents that create an opposing magnetic field, pushing the external field out.
*   **Result:** This expulsion creates a repulsive force, providing the initial **levitation** (lift) against gravity.

### B. Flux Pinning (Locking/Stability)
Flux pinning is the "locking" mechanism that gives the levitation its stability. This occurs only in **Type II superconductors** in a specific range of magnetic fields.
*   **Mechanism:** In this state, the magnetic field is too strong to be completely expelled (violating the Meissner state), so it penetrates the superconductor in discrete, quantized bundles called **magnetic flux tubes** (or vortices).
*   **The Lock:** These flux tubes become **trapped, or "pinned,"** at microscopic defects and impurities in the material's crystal structure. The superconductor resists any movement because moving would require the trapped flux lines to move, which costs energy.
*   **Result:** The object becomes **stably locked** in three dimensions (up/down, forward/back, rotationally) relative to the external magnetic field. This stability is so strong that the superconductor can be suspended *below* a magnet or even be turned upside down without falling.
